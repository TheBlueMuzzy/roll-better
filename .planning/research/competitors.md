## Detailed Analyses

### 1. Dice Forge (Libellud, 2017)

**Core Loop:** Each round, ALL players roll (even on opponents' turns -- "divine blessing"). Spend resources to buy new die faces from the Sanctuary -- physically pry off an old face and snap on a new one. Sun/moon shards buy Heroic Feat cards for VP and powers. 9-10 rounds, highest glory wins. 30-45 min.

**Art Style:** Vibrant mythological Greek/fantasy. High production -- removable translucent dice faces.

**Monetization:** ~$35-45 retail + "Rebellion" expansion (~$25). Free on BGA ($4/month premium).

**What Works:** Physical face-swapping is deeply satisfying and unique. "Divine blessing" keeps everyone engaged. Engine-building creates visible progression. Accessible yet strategic. Short play time.

**What Fails:** Downtime during dice modification decisions. Limited player interaction. Optimal strategies become apparent. Only 2-4 players. Physical faces can be fiddly.

**Relevance:** Closest spiritual ancestor. The core fantasy of "upgrading your dice" is proven compelling. Roll Better improves by making crafting faster (digital), adding direct PvP, enabling more players.

### 2. Dicey Dungeons (Terry Cavanagh, 2019)

**Core Loop:** Choose from six characters, each with unique play style. Navigate dungeon, fight enemies in turn-based combat. Roll dice, place onto equipment cards. Beat the boss. 20-30 min runs. 36 distinct challenge configurations.

**Art Style:** Bright surreal '90s cartoon -- characters are anthropomorphic dice. Pop-up book quality. Chipzel soundtrack.

**Monetization:** $5-15 premium. No microtransactions. Free "Reunion" DLC.

**What Works:** Each character feels like a different game. Charming personality. Information always visible. Short sessions. 90% positive Steam.

**What Fails:** No multiplayer. Limited replay value vs other roguelikes. Feels "solved" after 10-20 hours. Small equipment pools.

**Relevance:** Proves dice-as-core-mechanic sustains a full game with charming presentation. Depth must come from combinations, not just content.

---

### 3. Balatro (LocalThunk, 2024)

**Core Loop:** Play poker hands to beat escalating score targets. Between rounds, buy Joker cards (passive modifiers), Tarot cards (modify cards), Planet cards (level up hands). Build synergies for exponential multipliers. 20-40 min runs.

**Art Style:** Retro pixel art with CRT filter, psychedelic color shifts, synthwave aesthetic. Clean card designs. Explosive number animations.

**Monetization:** $15 premium. No microtransactions. 5M+ copies sold.

**What Works:** Poker hands = zero tutorial needed. "Modify your luck" core fantasy. Escalation from 200 to 2,000,000 feels thrilling. Perfect run length. Enormous emergent strategy from Joker combos. Solo dev mega-hit.

**What Fails:** No multiplayer (frequently requested). Can feel opaque when you lose. Presentation polarizing for some. Late-game can be repetitive.

**Relevance:** THE most important reference. Proved "modify your luck" is mainstream-appealing. Key lessons: (1) familiar baseline mechanic, (2) let players break the system spectacularly, (3) make numbers go absurdly high, (4) dopamine is in ANTICIPATION before the result. Absence of multiplayer = explicit opportunity.

### 4. Slice & Dice (tann, 2022-2024)

**Core Loop:** Party of 5 heroes, each a six-sided die. Faces show abilities. Roll all 5, assign against enemies. Limited rerolls. Between fights: new heroes, upgrade faces, gain items. 20-level dungeon, 30-45 min. 100+ hero classes.

**Art Style:** Minimalist pixel art -- clean, information-dense, functional.

**Monetization:** $8.99 premium. Free web demo on itch.io. 96% positive Steam.

**What Works:** Dice faces AS abilities -- elegant. Incredible depth from class synergies. Perfect information on enemies. One of best roguelikes ever. 100 classes, 30 dice types, 100 relics. Free web demo drives conversions.

**What Fails:** No multiplayer. Overwhelming for new players (100 classes). Polarizing visuals. Difficulty spikes. No narrative.

**Relevance:** Proves dice faces themselves can be the entire design space. "Dice face = ability" model is closest to dice-face crafting. Free web demo model worth considering.

---

### 5. Yahtzee with Buddies (Scopely)

**Core Loop:** Classic Yahtzee -- roll 5 dice, keep/reroll up to 3 times, fill scorecard. Async PvP. Seasonal events, daily challenges, cosmetics. 5-10 min matches.

**Monetization:** F2P aggressive: extra rolls for purchase, ads for bonuses, "Prize Pass" subscription, energy systems. ~$80K/month revenue.

**What Works:** Async multiplayer perfect for casual. Yahtzee ruleset universally known. Social features drive retention.

**What Fails:** Pay-to-win perception dominant. Players believe dice are "rigged." Reduced free resources over time. No depth beyond standard Yahtzee. Aggressive monetization = negative reviews.

**Relevance:** THE ANTI-MODEL. Proves demand for casual online dice games but shows exactly how NOT to monetize. "Rigged dice" perception is a critical warning -- Roll Better must be transparently fair.

### 6. King of Tokyo (Richard Garfield / IELLO, 2011)

**Core Loop:** Choose a monster. Roll 6 custom dice Yahtzee-style. Faces: Attack, Heal, Energy, VP (1/2/3). Three-of-a-kind numbers scores VP. Energy buys Power cards. Being "in Tokyo" gives bonus VP but makes you a target. Win at 20 VP or last standing. 20-30 min.

**Art Style:** Bold cartoony Japanese kaiju. Comic-book style. Custom oversized embossed dice.

**What Works:** Elegant design. Push-your-luck every roll. Territory mechanic adds drama. Power cards create replayability. Great at 2-6 players. Family-friendly but adults enjoy it. Under 30 min.

**What Fails:** Player elimination. Loses tension at 2 players. Uneven power card balance. Limited strategic depth for serious gamers.

**Relevance:** Perfected "dice as shared social experience." The groans, cheers, and tension -- this is the emotional target. Dice drama IS social. Multiplayer isn't optional; it's the whole point.

### 7. Dice Throne (Roxley Games, 2018+)

**Core Loop:** Pick asymmetric hero with unique dice and ability board. Roll 5 Yahtzee-style. Combos activate hero abilities. Spend Combat Points on cards to modify rolls, upgrade abilities, counter opponents. Reduce opponent to 0 HP. ~30 min.

**Art Style:** Lively animated fantasy/comic. Each hero has distinct visual identity. Premium component design.

**Monetization:** Hero pair sets ($20-30) or battle chests ($50-80). **Dice Throne Digital: Coming 2026, F2P, cross-play PC/Mobile/VR.** Most direct upcoming competitor.

**What Works:** "Main your character" identity. Tons of dice mitigation through cards. Highly competitive but accessible. Active tournament scene. "Full of dopamine triggers."

**What Fails:** Physical-only until 2026 digital. Expensive to collect. Some matchup imbalance. No persistent progression.

**Relevance:** Dice Throne Digital (2026) is the most direct future competitor. Roll Better differentiates through dice CRAFTING (Dice Throne heroes have fixed dice) and browser accessibility (no download required).

---

### 8. Random Dice (111%, 2019)

**Core Loop:** Merge dice on grid to create stronger towers. Dice = towers with different abilities. Enemies march in lanes. PvP: both defend simultaneously, longer survivor wins. 3-10 min matches.

**Monetization:** F2P gacha. Dice types from random chests. Premium currency. Battle pass. ~$80K/month.

**What Works:** Quick matches. Merging is tactile. Regular updates. Co-op mode. Deep meta-game.

**What Fails:** Balance problems (overpowered dice). Gacha frustrates F2P. P2W at high ranks. Not really a "dice game" -- tower defense with dice skin.

**Relevance:** "Dice + PvP + mobile" generates significant revenue, but gacha creates toxicity. Merge mechanic could inspire crafting UI. Real-time PvP with dice works, but balance is critical.

---

### 9. Liar's Dice / Perudo (Traditional)

**Core Loop:** Hidden dice under cups. Bid on total dice across all players. Raise bid or call "Liar!" Wrong caller/bidder loses a die. Last standing wins. 10-20 min.

**What Works:** Pure social deduction. Scales 2-10 players. Zero learning curve. Creates incredible tension. Works for all ages.

**What Fails:** Player elimination. Late game = pure luck. No progression. Digital versions lack physical tension. Most web implementations are bare-bones.

**Relevance:** Proves browser-based multiplayer dice games exist and people play them, but all are simple traditional game ports. Nobody has built a polished, progression-driven dice-crafting browser game.

---

### 10. Knucklebones (from Cult of the Lamb, 2022)

**Core Loop:** Two players, each with 3x3 grid. Roll single die, place in column. Matching values in same column multiply score. Same value in opponent's matching column DESTROYS their dice. Highest score when grid fills. 2-5 min.

**What Works:** Learn in 30 seconds. Meaningful decisions every turn. Destruction mechanic is satisfying. Multiplier scoring feels thrilling. Incredibly fast. Spawned multiple fan-made browser versions -- proof of demand.

**What Fails:** Very light -- no progression or meta-game. Limited depth after many plays. Luck-heavy. Fan sites are unpolished. No ranked matchmaking.

**Relevance:** Strongest proof-of-concept for browser-based competitive dice games. Multiple fan sites exist because demand is real. Scoring multipliers (matching values compound) directly relevant to dice-face crafting.

## Browser-Based Dice Multiplayer Landscape

**Existing:** Board Game Arena (11M+ users, hosts Dice Forge/King of Tokyo), Knucklebones.io/+, LiarsBar.io, Bloob.io, KDice, cardgames.io.

**The gap:** Every browser dice game is either a digital port of a physical game or a simple traditional dice game. No one has built an original, designed-for-digital, dice-crafting browser game with progression and competitive multiplayer.

## Dice Game Trends 2025-2026

1. **Dice-face customization emerging:** "Die in the Dungeon" (97% Steam), "Pip My Dice" -- concept gaining momentum
2. **Hybrid digital-tabletop:** Board games going digital aggressively (Dice Throne Digital 2026)
3. **Premium physical dice trending:** LED/metal/resin dice as collectibles -- players have emotional attachment to dice
4. **Roguelike fatigue vs. multiplayer hunger:** Single-player market saturated; multiplayer is #1 requested missing feature
5. **Browser games resurgence:** Market projected $9.07B by 2030; 47% now integrate real-time multiplayer
6. **Kidult market:** Adults 18-65 fastest-growing board game demographic
